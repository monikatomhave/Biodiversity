The belly button is one of the habitats closest to us, and yet it remains relatively unexplored.

The microbes inhabiting our navels and the factors that might influence the microscopic life calling this protected, moist patch of skin home have been collected and documented, spurring on even more conversation about the beneficial roles microbes play in our daily lives.

Hundreds of eager navel-gazers participated in the study, donating samples at in-person events where the volunteers participating in the study twirled a sterile swab in their belly buttons to collect their microbes.

There were two things done with the swabs:
1.) Each individual’s sample was plated and let it grow for a couple days, creating a microbial ‘portrait’ unique to each participant.
2.)Basic molecular biology was used to extract and sequence the DNA from the microbes collected from each belly button. Looking specifically at the 16S rRNA gene, a gene that’s extremely variable across different strains of microbes. It provides a molecular “fingerprint” that is being use to distinguish one species from another. With the help of special software, the microbial sequence data was turned into meaningful results: lists of species (and their relative abundances) for each belly button.

Using this data, the basic questions were explored about the diversity of microbes in belly buttons:
What and how many species live in the belly button?
Why are some species common in many belly buttons and (most) others are rare?
What factors determine the number and kinds of microbes we see in individual belly buttons?
